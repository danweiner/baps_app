Leo Mendoza added 5 new photos.
March 20 at 7:03am · Busan, South Korea ·

Today at BAPS, I go to see something that one can only say "Well, how about that...?"

Set up: During volunteer dog walking, we have a predetermined path to go.... Every Sunday, we have our same route. From time to time, the elderly residents of the area come out to yell at the volunteers, because that's what elderly people do when they see foreigner with dogs. We stopped fighting with them years ago, telling volunteers to just move along and ignore them, because that's what happens in Korea. To over-simplify it, old people have a free pass, where they get to yell ay anyone (Korean or foreign) as they please and everyone just lets them, because they are old. So, I tell volunteers, just walk away.

However, today a certain old man who has been a thorn in our side for years, really got belligerent and aggressive to a couple of our ladies. how aggressive? It got to the point where the mighty Claire Beattie was scared enough to call me for help. Now, this elderly guy is a man whose hand I'd never shake. He's a dog meat farmer. He openly has dozens and dozens of dogs caged in his property, in full view of the street, to be butchered.

I did what Leo does, which is rush out in my full anger... and as fortune would have, the local police patrol was just passing by. I sternly asked the two officers to come assist me, and they did so.

The old man proceeded to yell at the cops too, and the basis of his argument was that these foreigners with dogs (yes, he specifically said foreigners) should not walk on the street in front of his property, because it made his dogs bark, and he didn't want to be disturbed.

I naturally, laid onto them how what we are doing is perfectly legal, the street is a public space, I have been paying taxes in Korea for 16 years, and all of these foreigners also pay taxes. As such, we have as much right to use this road, which is public property as anyone, and that guy has no right to tell us to get off of it, much less to disturb the peace, yell at women, and frighten children.

At this point we all need to pause.

In many situations, Korea is not a welcoming place to foreigners when confrontation with the locals is involved, let's not kid ourselves about that. In the aforementioned 16 years I've had multiple confrontations over various topics with older Koreans who disapprove of my being here, and even had occasion to interact with the police. That was mostly in the early years, but the truth is that EVERY SINGLE time, the cops sided with the Korean person against me, or at best just said "move along". I know of many other foreigners who have been placed on the automatic wrong for just not being Korean.

I did my best to keep my voice at a civil but stern tone, fully prepared to hold my ground against the cops and the old guy. You have to do that. If I give them an inch, it won't be long before we would have to just not walk on the streets anymore. And I'm not the sort to meekly let my rights be trampled.

This is were things got strange. As the argument continued, the cops slowly sided on my side, and I saw them going from giving me stern looks, to slowly moving over to my side. I explained to them that we are a charity, we have a dog shelter, we all are professionals, and we have been doing this for 8 years.

Convinced that we were in the right, the cops just ordered the old man to get back in his house and never confront us again. then, right in front of him, one officer gave me his phone number and said "call me if he ever gives you trouble again."

Once the issue was settled, the cops started asking a lot of questions about why we do this. They were genuinely interested, and seemed quite impressed that we would all come from Busan, Ulsan, Geoje and other areas. One them was really interested in what we did for dogs. It was all quite... well... "foreign" to him.

I invited them to come into BAPS, and gave them a tour of the whole shelter, while explaining it all to them. They asked where we get the money, and when we told them that it was all donations, with no company or government support, they were very impressed that we would care for Korean dogs so much. We talked about a wide range of dog issues, including about dog meat. They nodded when I said that I respect Korea's traditions, and rather than saying anything against it, I just wanted to show example of a different way to interact with dogs.

When we pointed out the donation jar, this officer got his walled out, and put 20,000 won in (about 20 USD). He said he was impressed, and that what we do is very helpful to the community. He repeated that if we have any problem to call him.

How about that?

Congratulations to the Korean National Police. This officer and his partner represented them well as international ambassadors, and reminded us that even though there are dog meat people that wish us ill and stand in diametric opposition to what we do, there are also intelligent, respectful Koreans who are open to joining us in making a better world for dogs. Thank you to the Jucheon-Myeon local police station, and you have our best wishes and hopes that you all stay safe!

PS- I just gotta pat myself on the back for having conducted the entire operation in Korean language! Proud of my hard earned Korean skills smile emoticon

Busan Abandoned Pet Sanctuary

What happens to dogs that foreigners abandon when they go home?

This pup is a store-bought purebred American Cocker Spaniel. He belonged to a male English teacher from USA that decided it wasn't worth the effort to take the dog home. The dog was left in the reluctant care of a young Korean lady who has no clue or inclination for caring after a dog.

I had to make a choice. The woman gave an ultimatum that the dog had to leave. We all know what happens to Spaniels at Korean p...ounds. They almost never get adopted, so when an owner turns them in, it is immediate euthanasia.

As many of you know, BAPS is still overpopulated and spending far more money than gets donated. We are not in a position to intake dogs.... But Jin and I can't let a spaniel get put down in front of us. This is the breed that launched us into dog rescue.

Please someone adopt him. He is 1.5 years old, fully vaccinated, neutered, friendly, and he is also scared and confused.
